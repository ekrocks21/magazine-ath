Nineteen nations. Nineteen ships. Every one of them will aim for Sandy Hook Bay before they see Manhattan. We will be watching from the bluff.

Above the Hook · Spring 2026 · The Local Lookout

There is a moment, when approaching New York Harbor from the Atlantic, when the coast resolves out of the haze and you know where you are. The hills above Sandy Hook. The lighthouse that has burned there without interruption since 1764. The same mark that Giovanni da Verrazzano described in 1524, that Henry Hudson noted in 1609, that two million immigrants fixed their eyes on as they arrived at Ellis Island. This July, it will be the first thing seen by the crews of some of the most extraordinary sailing vessels on earth.

On July 3rd and 4th, 2026, the Port of New York and New Jersey will host the largest gathering of tall ships in modern history — a six-day maritime celebration of America's 250th anniversary that officials expect to draw eight to ten million spectators. More than fifty Class A and Class B tall ships from thirty countries will transit New York Harbor, joining fifty allied and U.S. naval vessels, the Queen Mary 2, and thousands of pleasure boats on a parade route stretching from the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge to the George Washington Bridge.

Every one of them passes through Sandy Hook Bay first. Every one of them sails past the hills of Highlands before they see Manhattan. From the Harbor Observatory, we will track them from the moment they appear on AIS.

What follows is the fleet — or rather, the portion of it that represents the nations and ships in the images handed to us by the Sail4th organizers. Nineteen vessels, nineteen stories. The oldest was launched in 1931. The newest in 2017. Together they carry crews and cadets from every ocean. On July 4th, the USCG Barque Eagle leads them through the gate.

Italy · Italian Navy
Class A · Full-Rigged Ship · Est. 1931
Amerigo Vespucci
La Spezia, Italy

Called "the most beautiful ship in the world" — not once, but twice, by American aircraft carriers that encountered her at sea. The USS Independence radioed the compliment in 1962. In 2022, the USS George H.W. Bush confirmed it: "You are still, after sixty years, the most beautiful ship in the world." Built in 1931 to resemble an 18th-century warship of the line, the Amerigo Vespucci's black hull is painted with two white stripes that recall the gun decks of the era she evokes. She has sailed continuously for nearly a century, training cadets of the Italian Naval Academy in the seamanship that has not fundamentally changed since the age of sail. Her motto, drawn from Leonardo da Vinci: "Not who begins, but he who perseveres."

Class A · Four-Masted Barquentine · Est. 1946
Esmeralda
Valparaíso, Chile

The second-largest tall ship in the world and the largest in this fleet. Built in Cádiz, Spain, in 1946, Esmeralda carries the largest sail area of any vessel coming to New York — 30,892 square feet across four masts. She has visited more than 300 ports on all five oceans. Her sister ship is the Juan Sebastián de Elcano of Spain. The Chilean Navy's training vessel and goodwill ambassador, she embarks 90 midshipmen alongside her permanent crew of 300. When she moves through Sandy Hook Bay, she will be visible from the Highlands bluff long before her hull clears the horizon.

Class A · Full-Rigged Ship · Est. 1963
ARA Libertad
Buenos Aires, Argentina

One of the largest and fastest tall ships on earth. ARA Libertad holds the world record for the fastest Atlantic crossing by a sail training vessel — 8 days, 12 hours from Cape Race, Newfoundland to the Dursey Island lighthouse off Ireland. Built in 1963 at the Río Santiago Shipyard in Ensenada, Argentina, she carries 28,545 square feet of sail across three masts. With a crew of 24 officers, 187 sailors, and 150 cadets, she is the Argentine Navy's flagship training vessel and one of the most recognized tall ships in the world. Her name means Liberty.

USCGC Eagle
U.S. Coast Guard · New London, CT

America's tall ship and the only active duty sailing vessel in the U.S. military. Built in Hamburg, Germany in 1936 as the Horst Wessel, she was taken by the United States as a war prize in 1946 and commissioned as Eagle. She has trained Coast Guard cadets ever since. On July 4th, 2026, Eagle will lead the International Parade of Sail into New York Harbor — the same role she has played at every major OpSail event in American history.

United States
Barque · 1877
Elissa
Texas Seaport Museum · Galveston, TX

The oldest ship in the fleet by more than half a century. Built in Aberdeen, Scotland in 1877 by Alexander Hall and Sons, Elissa is a three-masted iron barque that carried cargo across the world's oceans for decades before being rescued from a Greek breakers yard in 1975. Restored to sailing condition by the Galveston Historical Foundation, she is a National Historic Landmark and one of only a handful of 19th-century merchant sailing ships still capable of sailing under her own power.

Named for Sagres — the southwestern tip of Portugal from which Henry the Navigator launched the Age of Discovery in the 15th century. Built in 1918 as a cargo schooner in Rotterdam, the Oosterschelde sailed the Baltic and North Sea trade routes for decades before being restored to her original condition in 1992. From the Bluff · The Harbor Observatory
Above the Hook · Harbor Observatory

Watching from the hills above it all

The Sandy Hook Harbor Observatory tracks every vessel transiting New York Harbor in real time from the hills of Highlands — an Axis 4K PTZ camera on the bluff, an AIS receiver reading every ship's position and identity, an AI system writing the story of each vessel as it passes.

For Sail4th 250, we will be watching the entire week. Every tall ship that passes Sandy Hook Bay will be captured, identified, and documented in the Observatory archive. The fleet profiles you have read in this issue will appear live on the Observatory as each vessel enters the harbor.

The best view in America is from this bluff. The best view on this bluff is through the Observatory camera. On July 4th, when Eagle leads the parade through Sandy Hook Bay, we will be live.

The Parade Route

July 3: Tall ships parade through the East River from upper Manhattan toward downtown.

July 4: The full International Parade of Sail begins near the Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge, moving north through the Hudson River. Eagle leads.

Sandy Hook Bay: Every ship transits here first. The Observatory captures them all as they pass the Highlands bluff — before the crowds, before Manhattan, before the parade begins.
