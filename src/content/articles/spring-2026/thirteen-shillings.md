# Thirteen Shillings

*In 1680, a Quaker merchant from Yorkshire paid less than a pound sterling for Sandy Hook. What he bought, and what he believed he was buying, were two very different things.*

Thirteen shillings, in the spring of 1680, would have bought you something like this: two good wool coats. A pair of boots. Eleven pounds of sugar shipped out of Barbados. A decent horse, if you negotiated. A week's worth of lodging at a respectable inn in London. A small cask of Madeira.

It would also, as it turned out, have bought you Sandy Hook.

The deed is a single page of ink on paper, now held in the archives of the Monmouth County Clerk's office in Freehold, its handwriting a disciplined seventeenth-century English secretary's hand, its edges soft from three and a half centuries of careful handling. It is dated the eighth of April, 1680. It records the transfer of a long, curving peninsula of sand — roughly six miles from base to tip, somewhere between six hundred and fifteen hundred acres depending on the tide — from a group of Lenape signatories named in the deed as "Vowavapon, Tocus, Popomora, Mishacoing, Quereman, Yawpoham, and Norwenoch" to one Richard Hartshorne of Portland Point, yeoman, late of Hathersage in Derbyshire, England.

The price, spelled out plainly in the body of the document, is thirteen shillings.

To understand what was purchased that April, and what was not, you have to understand the three people in the room: Richard Hartshorne, the Lenape, and the peninsula itself.

---

Richard Hartshorne was thirty-nine years old in 1680, and he had been in the New World for about seven. He was born in 1641, in the village of Hathersage in the Peak District, to a minor Anglican gentry family of the kind that produced country parsons and small landowners in rough ratio. He left the Church of England as a young man and joined the Religious Society of Friends — the Quakers — which in that period was not a social affiliation but an existential one. To be a Quaker in seventeenth-century England was to be, at best, professionally locked out of the universities, the military, the courts, and most professions, and at worst, imprisoned. His older brother Hugh was jailed in Nottinghamshire for refusing to take an oath. Richard appears to have taken the hint.

He came first to Barbados, which in the 1660s and 1670s was absorbing a steady trickle of English Quakers — some as planters, some as merchants, some as missionaries. From Barbados he moved, in 1669, to the Monmouth Patent, a tract of central New Jersey that had been opened to English settlement four years earlier under a proprietor's grant from James, Duke of York. The Patent guaranteed, among other things, freedom of worship and the right to purchase land directly from the indigenous inhabitants. Both of these provisions were, by the standards of the day, unusual. Both of them mattered to a man like Hartshorne.

He built a house at Portland Point, on the north shore of what is now Atlantic Highlands, overlooking the Navesink. The house was substantial — a plastered stone affair with a good view of the water — and it still stood, in some form, into the early nineteenth century. He planted orchards. He ran livestock. He raised four sons and two daughters who lived to adulthood, and he buried, as most men of his time did, several others. He served, repeatedly, as a member of the East Jersey Assembly. He was, by every available measure, a successful colonial yeoman of the first generation.

He was also, by every available measure, genuinely different from most of his English neighbors in one respect: he appears to have taken seriously, rather than rhetorically, the idea that the land he was settling on belonged to other people first.

---

The Lenape — "the people," in their own language — had been in the lower Hudson, the Raritan Bay, and the Sandy Hook region for at least ten thousand years before any European arrived. By the time Richard Hartshorne came into their country, their world was in the middle of a long, slow-motion collapse.

Smallpox had arrived in the 1630s and had already carried off, by conservative estimate, something on the order of half the Lenape population of the coast. The Pequot War in 1637 and Kieft's War in 1643 — both fought against Algonquian peoples closely related to the Lenape — had demonstrated what European neighbors could do if sufficiently provoked, or sufficiently unprovoked. Dutch and Swedish traders had introduced alcohol, metal tools, firearms, and the wampum economy, all of which had restructured Lenape material life in ways that no one fully understood yet. Beaver were gone from most of northern New Jersey. Deer were thinning. The old migratory cycles that had moved bands between the shore and the interior with the seasons were becoming harder to sustain.

And English-speaking farmers were arriving in increasing numbers.

The seven signatories named in the 1680 deed were, almost certainly, the surviving leadership of what remained of the Navesink band — a Lenape community that had traditionally summered on Sandy Hook to harvest shellfish, fish the bay, and hunt the marshes, and had wintered inland in the sheltered drainages of the Navesink and Shrewsbury rivers. Their territory had already been, formally or informally, sold or signed over in pieces by earlier treaties and deeds — to the Dutch, to the English, to other English. Sandy Hook, by 1680, was probably the last significant piece of unalienated Navesink land along the Monmouth coast.

What the thirteen-shilling price suggests — and here the historical reading gets difficult — is that the Lenape signatories did not believe they were selling the peninsula in the English sense of the word.

---

In seventeenth-century English law, a sale of land was a transfer of exclusive, inheritable ownership in perpetuity. The seller gave up the right to use, occupy, or return to the land. The buyer acquired the right to exclude. This was the meaning of *property*, and it was the meaning of *deed*, and it was, in the English legal imagination, the entire point of the transaction.

In Lenape thinking — as far as we can reconstruct it from the historical and ethnographic record — land was not alienable in this way. What could be transferred was the right to use land: to plant on it, to hunt on it, to build on it, to share it with another people. The idea of a piece of land permanently leaving a community, never to return, was not so much rejected as unimaginable. The land was part of the community. The community was part of the land. You might as well sell the wind.

When the Navesink signatories put their marks to Hartshorne's deed in April 1680, they were almost certainly understanding the transaction as a use-right agreement. Thirteen shillings, in goods or its equivalent, was perhaps a generous gift in acknowledgement of an ongoing accommodation — the kind of gift a new neighbor might give for the privilege of grazing stock on the Hook through the summer.

When Hartshorne put his own signature to the same document, he was understanding it as the permanent, total, exclusive acquisition of a peninsula.

Both men were acting in good faith. Both men had a legal tradition behind them. Both men were, in the semantic sense, right. But they were not describing the same event.

---

For the first decade after the deed, the misunderstanding did not surface, because the two understandings happened to produce the same practical outcome. Hartshorne did not attempt to exclude the Lenape from Sandy Hook. They continued to summer there. He ran cattle on the Hook, which was fine; they fished the bay, which was fine; periodically he or one of his sons would take a boat over to check on the herd, and periodically the Navesink would paddle over to the mainland to trade, and the arrangement, if it was an arrangement, held.

Then, beginning in the 1690s, the trouble started.

It came in the form of outsiders. English ships putting into the Hook for water, firewood, or provisions had begun to take livestock without permission. The Lenape, hunting on the peninsula, had reportedly killed or driven off some of Hartshorne's cattle. Other English settlers, reading the deed as an exclusive grant, were beginning to question Hartshorne's tolerance of what they saw as trespass. A 1697 petition — preserved, remarkably, in Hartshorne's own surviving papers — records his complaint to the East Jersey Proprietors that "the Indians doe much mischief upon my land at Sandy Hook," and requests the formal right to remove them.

The request was granted. The Navesink were, over the following decade, gradually pressed off Sandy Hook entirely. Those who did not join the larger Lenape migration westward toward the Susquehanna — a migration that would continue for the next seventy years, eventually carrying most of the eastern Lenape to Ohio, Indiana, and finally Oklahoma — merged into the mixed communities of surviving coastal Algonquians that can still, today, be traced in places like the Ramapough Lenape Nation of northern New Jersey.

By 1715, Sandy Hook was, in the English sense, entirely and unambiguously Richard Hartshorne's.

---

Hartshorne lived until 1722. His will, which survives, divided the Hook among his sons, along with the house at Portland Point and the other family holdings. The peninsula remained Hartshorne family property, in various configurations, for most of the eighteenth century. It was used for grazing, wrecking, and — after 1746 — the original Sandy Hook Lighthouse, which still stands and is the oldest continuously operating lighthouse in the United States.

In the early nineteenth century, the federal government began acquiring the Hook for military purposes. By the end of the Civil War, most of it was in federal hands. Fort Hancock was established there in 1895. The army held the peninsula until 1974, when it was transferred to the National Park Service and became part of the Gateway National Recreation Area.

Today, Sandy Hook is public land. It is free to enter, free to walk, free to fish. On any given summer afternoon there are probably more than ten thousand people on it. A great many of them are swimming.

None of them, when they buy a soft-serve from the snack bar at Area E, are paying thirteen shillings.

---

You can still visit Richard Hartshorne's grave. It is in a small, overgrown family burying ground on the grounds of the house he built at Portland Point, which is now private property overlooking Sandy Hook Bay in Atlantic Highlands. The stone is modest, as Quaker markers tend to be, weathered by three centuries of salt air.

You can also, on a clear day, walk to the tip of Sandy Hook and look back across the water at the hills he once farmed on. From the Hook, the Highlands rise up in the west exactly as Robert Juet saw them in 1609, and exactly as the Navesink saw them for ten millennia before that. They have not moved. They will not move.

What moved, in the spring of 1680, was something smaller and harder to name — a piece of paper that carried, in two different languages that were not fully translatable into each other, two different descriptions of what had just occurred.

Thirteen shillings.

A very good land, as somebody had already said.

A pleasant land to see.

And, for a time, a land to share.
