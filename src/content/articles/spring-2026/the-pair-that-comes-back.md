# The Pair That Comes Back

*Ospreys almost disappeared from this coast. They didn't. Every spring, one pair returns to the same platform at the edge of the Hook and begins, again, the work of making more of themselves.*

The platform sits on a telephone pole at the edge of Sandy Hook where the marsh grass gives way to open water and the bay widens north toward the channel. It is not a remarkable object. A square of weathered plywood, maybe four feet on a side, bolted to the top of a pole that someone from the Audubon Society or the park service or the state put up a long time ago — I've never been able to figure out which, and at some point the answer stops mattering. It has been there as long as anyone I've asked can remember. It has, for at least the last six springs, held a nest.

In the winter, the platform is empty. The nest itself — a messy tangle of sticks, marsh grass, pieces of plastic line, occasional lengths of fishing rope that were not put there by any human — stays in place through the storms, mostly. Some years it comes apart and has to be rebuilt from scratch. Most years it holds.

In late March, the pair returns.

---

You can set your watch by them, almost. The first week of April in a warm year. The last week of March if the winter has been mild. I have made a quiet habit, for several springs now, of walking down to the edge of the marsh in the last days of March to check. For a week or ten days there is nothing. The platform sits empty. The nest material rots a little. A crow occasionally lands on the rim and then leaves, finding it insufficient to its needs.

And then one morning there is a bird.

Sometimes it's the male; sometimes the female. They do not arrive together. Whichever one gets there first stands on the platform for a long time, alone, looking at nothing in particular. I do not know what an osprey thinks. But if I had to read it into the posture of the bird, I would say it was checking. Making sure the platform was still here. Making sure the marsh was still here. Making sure it had, in fact, found the place it was looking for.

And then the other one comes.

---

Ospreys are one of the few species I know of that reliably return to the same nest, year after year, across thousands of miles of migration. The pair that comes back to this platform at the edge of the Hook in April has, by that point, already flown from somewhere in South America. One might have wintered in coastal Venezuela; the other in the Amazon basin; they do not travel together. They arrive separately, within a few days of each other, having each crossed the Caribbean and the Gulf of Mexico and the length of the United States, having eaten fish the whole way, and they land on this specific platform in this specific salt marsh as if no time at all has passed.

The scale of that is difficult to hold in your head.

There is no map of it anywhere in the bird. Or rather, there is a map, but we don't understand it. Some combination of magnetic sense, celestial cues, coastline memory, and something else we cannot yet name, held in a skull the size of a walnut, brings two animals separately across a hemisphere to a point on a telephone pole in New Jersey. They land. They recognize each other. And they begin, without ceremony, to behave as if they had never been apart.

---

The first thing they do is fix the nest.

An osprey nest, after a winter of storms, is never quite as the pair left it. The top layer of grass has blown off. Sticks are missing. A northeaster may have taken the whole south side of it. The birds' first week back is devoted to carpentry. The male, typically, does the heavy lifting — he will fly in from a quarter mile away carrying a branch nearly his own length, banking hard to set it in place, working it into the existing structure with his beak. The female rearranges. Weaves. Occasionally she stands in the center of the nest and turns in a slow circle, pressing down with her body, as if testing whether it will hold. It is impossible to watch this without thinking about people building a house.

The work takes about a week. Some years longer. By the second week of April the nest has a visible bowl in its top surface, and then one morning you arrive at the marsh and the female is sitting low, and you know the eggs are down.

They incubate for about thirty-nine days. During that time the female is almost always on the nest. The male fishes. This is the division of labor for most of the reproductive season: the female holds the center, the male brings food, and the system works because ospreys, alone among North American raptors, eat essentially nothing but fish.

---

That last fact is the thing that almost killed them.

In the middle decades of the twentieth century, the pesticide DDT moved through American waterways and into the bodies of American fish, and from the fish into the bodies of the birds that ate them. In ospreys, the DDT metabolite DDE interfered with the calcium metabolism that produces eggshells. The shells went thin. The females, incubating, broke their own eggs under their weight. For twenty-odd years, from the late 1950s into the early 1970s, osprey reproduction along the Atlantic coast collapsed. New Jersey's population fell from more than five hundred breeding pairs to fifty-three. Nests that had been active for generations went empty and stayed empty.

In 1972, the United States banned DDT.

The recovery was not fast. It took the DDT already in the food chain decades to dilute. It took the surviving ospreys years to re-establish nesting territories in places where the old generations had died off without reproducing. But slowly, and then less slowly, the birds came back. In the 1980s the state and various nonprofits began installing nesting platforms — wooden squares on telephone poles, exactly like the one at the mouth of the Shrewsbury — to give the recovering population safe places to nest in a landscape where the old snag trees were mostly gone. By 2010, New Jersey had over 500 breeding pairs again. By 2020, more than 700. The 2023 state census counted the highest number of active nests on record.

The pair that comes back to the platform at the edge of the Hook every April is part of that recovery. They do not know they are. They are simply doing what ospreys do, which is what ospreys have always done, and which they very nearly stopped doing inside the lifetime of people who are still alive today.

---

The eggs hatch in late May. The chicks are small, gray, damp-looking — nothing like the adults. For the first two weeks they are barely visible from the ground; you can tell they have hatched only because the female's posture on the nest changes, tilting forward as she feeds them, and because the male has started making more food runs, diving into the bay and coming back up with bunker or small bluefish held in his talons with the fish's head pointed always forward, into the wind, the way an osprey carries a fish and no other bird does.

By early July the chicks are large enough to stand up in the nest. You can see three heads, sometimes four, moving in and out of view. Ospreys typically lay three eggs; four is rare; in a good year at the edge of the Hook, all three or all four fledge.

In late July the first chick lifts off the nest. Not flies — lifts. It catches a thermal, rises a few feet above the rim, and then drops back down, startled. A day or two later, it does it again, longer. A week later it is circling the marsh clumsily, returning always to the platform, not yet knowing how to fish, waiting for the parents to bring food. The parents do, for another several weeks. Eventually the chicks leave and do not come back. Some of them, it is believed, will return to this exact stretch of coast in two or three years to breed, though which chicks and which stretch remains, like the magnetic map, something we have not fully decoded.

And then, in late August or early September, the adults leave too. Not together. The female tends to go first, as soon as the last chick is independent. The male stays another few weeks. By October the platform is empty again.

The nest holds through the winter. Mostly.

---

A friend asked me once why I find this particular cycle so moving. I didn't have an answer at the time. I think the answer now is that ospreys do something most of us would like to be able to do, which is to come home — not to an idea of home, but to a specific location, a specific platform at a specific bend in a specific river — and to find that home has held. That the work of the previous year has persisted. That the thing you made, you can begin again.

They do not know any of this. They are just birds.

But they come back.
