# The Forty-Five Minutes

*There is a ferry that leaves Atlantic Highlands every morning and nobody who rides it ever takes it for granted. A round-trip across the most important piece of water in American history — and back.*

---

## I. Outbound

The boat leaves the dock at 7:42.

The people who ride this ferry every day do not need a schedule. They arrive at the terminal in the dim of morning with coffees in one hand and bags in the other, nod to the deckhand, step aboard, find their preferred seat by the window — everyone has one, and nobody takes anyone else's — and sit down. By the time the ropes come off, most of them are reading something. Some are not. Some of them, after all these years, still like to watch.

It is worth watching.

The Seastreak is a commuter boat, technically. It is rated for five hundred passengers and it makes the run from Atlantic Highlands to East 34th Street in forty-five minutes, port to port, for the price of a movie ticket and the cost of a river crossing. On paper it is a transportation decision. In practice — for those of us who live on this stretch of coast and have made the trip more than a few times — it is one of the strangest and most beautiful commutes in America.

You do not sit in traffic. You do not take a train. You do not breathe tunnel air. You cross a harbor instead.

The first thing the boat does is back out of the slip, turn its bow north, and push through the Atlantic Highlands breakwater into the open bay. The Highlands hills rise up on your left — the bluffs that Robert Juet wrote about in 1609, the ones that have been the landmark of this coast since before there was a country to land on. Mount Mitchill is there, the tallest. The Twin Lights, the old lighthouses, are there too, high up on the ridge, a pair of stone eyes over the entrance to the bay.

You do not see any of this if you do not know to look for it.

---

## II. The Hook

The boat clears the bay and the first thing you see on your right is Sandy Hook.

This is the peninsula that defines everything. Six miles of barrier spit — not a barrier island, as people sometimes say, because it is connected to the mainland at its southern end; a barrier *spit* — curving up into New York Harbor like a bent finger. Pointing. The way it has been pointing for at least ten thousand years.

At the tip of the Hook, tucked into the trees, is the oldest working lighthouse in the United States.

It is called, simply, the Sandy Hook Lighthouse. It was first lit on the eleventh of June, 1764. It predates the country that eventually inherited it by twelve years. In 1790, the new United States Senate — acting on a directive from President Washington — formally took federal ownership of the tower, and it has been in federal hands ever since. It was the first light immigrants saw when they arrived in New York Harbor. It is still lit. The Coast Guard keeps it now, as they have kept it since the Lighthouse Board was folded into the service in 1939.

You cannot quite see the lighthouse itself from the ferry — it sits set back from the water, screened by maritime forest — but you can see the top of it if you know where to look, and you can see the fort that grew up around it over the centuries, Fort Hancock, with its row of officers' houses and its abandoned gun batteries. From the early 1800s through the middle of the twentieth century, the American military fortified this spit of sand against every threat it could imagine. Spanish frigates. German battleships. Soviet bombers. None of them came. The forts were decommissioned. The Park Service took it over in 1974. Now the officers' houses are rented out, and the batteries are slowly going back to dune.

The ferry does not stop. The ferry never stops here. Sandy Hook slides past on the starboard rail, and the next thing you see, rising from the water like a piece of the harbor has solidified, is Romer Shoal.

---

It is an iron sparkplug. A lighthouse built on a concrete caisson sunk into a shoal in the middle of open water, painted white at the top and black at the bottom, with a lantern that still runs. It was first lit in 1898, though a light station has marked this shoal in one form or another since 1838. It is named for Colonel Wolfgang William Romer, a British military engineer who in 1700 was ordered by the Governor of New York to sound and chart the waters of New York Bay. He did. He produced the first reasonably accurate map of these approaches. His name ended up on a shoal he probably never set foot on, because the shoal was too dangerous to set foot on, which was the entire point.

Everything you are looking at out here — every buoy, every channel marker, every offset light — exists because this stretch of water is the front door to America, and it has been continuously worked, charted, dredged, and lit for more than three hundred years so that ships can come through it without dying.

Keep watching. On either side of Romer, the channel opens up.

The big ones arrive.

---

## III. The channel

Container ships five stories high. Oil tankers half the length of a city block. Car carriers like floating parking garages. LNG tankers with their domed holds. Bulk carriers stained red with rust and ore. They come in through the Ambrose Channel at eight or nine knots, and every one of them — every one — has a pilot aboard.

The Sandy Hook Pilots have been boarding ships entering New York Harbor since the late seventeenth century. The earliest pilotage laws in these waters date to 1694. The current association is the direct descendant of a trade that predates the American Revolution. A pilot climbs from a small fast pilot boat onto a rope ladder thrown down the side of a seven-hundred-foot tanker — in fog, in snow, at three in the morning, in any weather that does not actually forbid it — climbs forty feet up that ladder with a hand-held VHF and a briefcase full of charts, gets to the bridge, and takes the ship in. They do this because no captain in the world, however skilled, knows the Ambrose Channel and the tides of the Narrows and the current off Gravesend Bay the way the pilots do. The pilots live out here on a station boat twenty miles offshore. They rotate in shifts. Their sons and daughters do it now. They do it in the same families.

When you see a small pilot boat shoving between two tankers at the edge of the channel, what you are watching is a three-hundred-and-thirty-year-old institution going to work.

---

## IV. The islands

And then, on the port side, between the channel and Staten Island, two scruffy green patches of nothing.

You will miss them if no one tells you to look.

Their names are Hoffman and Swinburne. They are not on most maps that tourists read. They are not mentioned in most histories of New York. They are, technically, part of Gateway National Recreation Area — closed to the public, patrolled by the Park Service, visited by no one but the birds.

The federal government built these islands.

Not found them, not claimed them — *built* them. In the 1870s, the United States Quarantine Service needed a place to hold immigrants who arrived in New York Harbor carrying communicable diseases. The Castle Garden depot at the Battery was too close to the city. The old quarantine station on Staten Island had been burned down by local residents who did not want contagion on their shore. So the government dredged sand from the harbor, piled it onto two submerged shoals, and made islands out of nothing. Hoffman, the larger one, finished in 1873, was for observation. Swinburne, smaller, was for the sick.

If you arrived on a steamship at the port of New York in the last quarter of the nineteenth century, and you had a fever, you did not go to Ellis Island. You went here.

The sick went to Swinburne. The hospital ran there through the turn of the century. There is still a crematorium chimney standing on Swinburne — short, brick, anonymous — which was used for the bodies of immigrants who did not survive their first glimpse of America. You can see it from the ferry if you know where to look. The foundations of the hospital are still there. So is a barracks building. So is the sea wall.

Hoffman was observation. People who were exposed but not yet sick were held there for weeks at a time, to see which way they would go. The wind carried the sound of singing from Hoffman on certain nights — a thing that is recorded in the diaries of steamship passengers who were anchored off the quarantine on their way in, before they knew whether they would be released or rerouted.

Both islands ceased operations before World War I. The buildings fell in slowly. The sand piled up and blew off and piled up again. Today they are bird sanctuaries — herons, cormorants, gulls, a few harbor seals in the winter — and what remains of the quarantine hospital is something between a ruin and a sand dune.

A hundred and fifty years of silence. Six hundred yards off the port rail. Most of the passengers on this boat have never looked at them.

---

## V. The bridge

Past the islands, the harbor tightens.

The Verrazzano-Narrows Bridge comes up ahead of the boat like a wall. A mile wide. A thousand feet above the water at its towers. Four thousand two hundred and sixty feet of main span, which in 1964 was the longest suspension bridge in the world and held that record until 1981. The ferry runs right under it. You sit at the bow, and the shadow of the span crosses your face, and you feel small. You are supposed to.

The Narrows is where the harbor pinches. Every ship that has ever entered New York — every ship, across four hundred years — has had to squeeze through this exact gap. Henry Hudson's Halve Maen, with Juet at the rail, came through here in September 1609. The immigrants came through here. The Queen Mary came through here. The Liberty ships came through here, and the troop transports, and the tankers, and now the container ships with their stacks of steel boxes painted every color, bound for the Port of New York and New Jersey, which is the largest port on the East Coast and the third largest in the United States.

The bridge crosses the gap. The ferry passes beneath it. You lift your head as you go under, and you keep it lifted for a second after.

You clear the bridge. And the city shows up.

---

## VI. The city

The Statue of Liberty appears off the port rail, small from this distance, pale green in the morning sun. Lower Manhattan is straight ahead, the financial towers stacked up like the end of a ruler. The ferry banks to starboard and enters the East River, and the bridges start passing overhead — the Brooklyn Bridge, the Manhattan Bridge, the Williamsburg — each one from a different era of New York, each one lower and less graceful than the one before.

The boat slows. The dock at 34th Street comes up. The passengers gather their bags.

Forty-five minutes. You did not sit in traffic. You did not take a train. You crossed the harbor that built this city. You passed a lighthouse older than America. You passed islands most New Yorkers do not know exist.

You step off the boat onto 34th Street. You walk half a block to the avenue. You catch a cab uptown, or a subway downtown, and within ten minutes you are inside a day that looks like any other day in New York.

Nobody in the building you are walking into knows where you came from this morning. Nobody knows that you crossed Romer Shoal, that you went under the Verrazzano, that you looked at Hoffman Island and thought about the people held there in 1892. You do not tell them. There is no reason to. The knowledge is yours.

This is the deal the ferry makes with its regulars. It gives you the harbor in the morning. You give it the day.

---

## VII. Inbound

The 6:10 leaves Pier 11 and makes its return run in reverse.

The sun is different coming home. In winter it is already down when the boat shoves off, and the city pulls away behind you lit up from below, a long gold line against a slate-blue sky. In summer it is high and flat, and the harbor looks bleached. In spring — in the spring when you are reading this, when the ospreys have come back — the light hits the Verrazzano just right as the boat passes under it eastbound, and for about ninety seconds the whole bridge glows orange, and you remember why you ride this boat.

The inbound trip is quieter. People are tired. Some of them fall asleep. The commuters who were reading on the way in are looking out the window on the way home, because you have done the work, and the harbor is what you earned.

You pass the bridges in reverse. You pass Hoffman and Swinburne on your starboard side now, and they look smaller in the evening. You clear the Verrazzano. The harbor opens. The tankers are lit for the night. The pilots are still working — they will work all night — and you see their boats running under the tanker lights, small and fast.

Romer Shoal passes. Its lantern flashes. It has been flashing since 1898.

And then Sandy Hook rises on your left, low and dark, and behind it the Highlands hills, with the house lights coming on all the way up the ridge. Mount Mitchill is up there. The Twin Lights are up there. Your own house is up there, if you live where we live.

The ferry pushes past the tip of the Hook and enters the bay. The engines drop. The hull settles. You are inside the bay now — the home water — and you can see your dock.

Forty-five minutes ago, you were in Manhattan.

You did not sit in traffic. You did not take a train.

You crossed the harbor coming, and you crossed it going, and both directions are the same water, and neither of them is the thing between the Highlands and New York.

It is the thing that connects them.

And you are home.
