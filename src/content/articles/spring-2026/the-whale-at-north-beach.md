# The Whale at North Beach

*A young female humpback washed ashore at Sandy Hook on the first of April. I drove out to see her. I did not look.*

---

She was first spotted at 7:50 in the morning. Floating, dead, just off the beach at Sandy Hook — somewhere between Fisherman's and Gunnison, in the stretch of shoreline where the dunes open out and the Atlantic comes in straight and unbroken off the open ocean. A walker saw her from the sand. A call went in to the Marine Mammal Stranding Center in Brigantine. Within half an hour the surf had brought her up onto the beach, and by the time the first responders arrived she was already out of the water, half on her side, still wet, twenty miles from where she had been born and an ocean away from where she should have been headed.

She was a young female. Thirty-five feet one inch long. Seventeen tons. A juvenile humpback, not yet an adult, in what the necropsy team would later describe as *good body condition* — well-fed, actively foraging, a full gastrointestinal tract at the time of death. She had been eating. Probably menhaden. Probably the same schools of menhaden that the ospreys were hunting on the same tide, from the other side of the food web.

On her right flank and along the right side of her head, the team found heavy bruising. Internal hemorrhaging consistent with blunt-force trauma. The kind of injury a whale sustains when a ship hits her.

On her flukes — the great tail that drives a humpback through the water — they found old scars from a line that had wrapped her and cut her and eventually been shed or worked off or cut free, years ago, somewhere in the North Atlantic. She had survived that one.

She did not survive the second one.

---

I drove out to Sandy Hook the next morning.

I want to be clear about why. I did not drive out because I needed to see her. I drove out because I had been making films and photographs of this coastline for seven months, and a whale had come ashore on the beach I walk most often, and it felt — in some way I could not entirely articulate — like the right thing to do was to be there. To witness. To take pictures. To make, in the language I was starting to understand I speak, something out of the loss.

The park road was quiet. The parking lot had a few cars and a Park Service truck with its lights off. The beach entrance was roped off. Beyond the rope I could see the shape on the sand, far enough away that it was more gesture than anatomy. I could see the orange and yellow of the stranding team's gear. I could see the tarp. I could not see her clearly, and I was not supposed to.

I stood at the rope for a long time.

Then I walked back to my truck, and I drove home, and I did not take any of the pictures I had driven out there to take.

---

What changed was small. I want to be careful about how I describe it because the last thing this piece needs is to be a story about my own moral growth, which is not the point and which I don't think I had.

What changed is that I looked at the rope, and I looked at what was behind it, and I understood for the first time that she was not a thing that had happened to the beach. She was a young female animal, delivered to Sandy Hook by an ocean that had also killed her, and around her were people whose job was to find out what had happened to her and then to take her away quietly. She was not a spectacle. She was a body being attended to by people who understood what she had been.

And the most generous thing I could do, standing on that sand with a camera and my own impulse to turn grief into content, was to not participate. To not cross the rope. To not document. To not post. To not make her death any more public than it already was going to be.

So I did not.

---

The Marine Mammal Stranding Center released a photograph that evening, on their Facebook page, for the people who needed to see. They released the preliminary necropsy findings on the third of April. She was, they wrote, in good condition. She had been feeding. Her death had been caused by a vessel. They thanked the Park Service, the volunteers, a construction company named Blue World that had brought equipment to move her, and the partner agencies — the Atlantic Marine Conservation Society, the MERR Institute — that had come up from further south to help with the examination. They did the work quietly and correctly and they did it in weather that made the work harder, and they put her in the ground.

She was the second whale they had handled in New Jersey this year. The first was a young male fin whale, found dead at Barnegat Light in February.

---

She was not the first humpback to die like this.

Since January of 2016, the Atlantic coast of the United States — from Maine to Florida — has been in what the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration officially calls an Unusual Mortality Event. An UME is a legal designation under the Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972; it means that the deaths of a given population of marine mammals have reached a level that is unexpected and cannot be explained by ordinary patterns. The humpback UME was declared in April 2017. It is now, in 2026, ten years old. It has not ended.

In the waters of the New York Bight — the shallow triangle of ocean bounded by Fire Island to the north and the Manasquan Inlet to the south, with Sandy Hook near the center of its western edge — more than sixty humpbacks have died since the UME began. Of the animals examined thoroughly enough to reach a finding, roughly forty percent have shown injuries caused by human activity. Most of those injuries are from ships. Some are from entanglement in fishing gear. A few are from both.

The ironic truth is that the humpback whales of the North Atlantic, as a population, are doing better than they have done in a century. The commercial whaling that reduced them nearly to extinction ended in the 1970s. Protection under the Endangered Species Act, and then later delisting when the population rebounded, returned humpbacks to the waters off the Northeast in numbers that no one alive had ever seen. In 2011, the nonprofit Gotham Whale — which tracks humpback sightings in the New York Bight from a whale-watch boat out of Staten Island — identified five individual whales in the waters around New York Harbor. By 2022, they had catalogued two hundred and fifty-seven. A Rutgers study published that same year found that humpbacks now stay in the Bight for an average of thirty-seven days per season. They are not passing through. They are living here.

They are living here because we have let the water get clean enough and we have let the menhaden come back and we have let the striped bass and the bluefish and the sand eels return — all the food that humpbacks eat — and the humpbacks, who have been pattern-matching this coast for millions of years, have come back to what was historically one of the great feeding grounds of the North Atlantic.

And now our ships are killing them.

---

The port complex of New York and New Jersey is the largest on the East Coast and the third-largest in the United States. More than four thousand commercial vessels enter and leave it every year. The Ambrose Channel — the deep-water channel that runs from the Atlantic into Lower New York Bay, passing within a mile of Sandy Hook on its way in — is one of the busiest shipping lanes in the Western Hemisphere. Vessel traffic has grown by roughly a third over the last five years. Ships have gotten larger. Ships have gotten faster. And the humpbacks, particularly the juveniles, who chase menhaden closer to shore than the adults do, are increasingly in the same water at the same time.

NOAA established voluntary speed restrictions in the early 2000s. Mandatory restrictions for vessels over sixty-five feet in certain zones came in 2008, primarily to protect the critically endangered North Atlantic right whale. The humpbacks, being more numerous and not listed as endangered, are not protected by the same rules. There is an argument to be made that they should be. There is an argument to be made that mandatory speed restrictions of ten knots in the approaches to New York Harbor, during the months when the whales are present, would save lives. The arguments have been made. The restrictions have not come.

The juvenile female humpback who washed ashore at Sandy Hook on the first of April, 2026, did not know any of this. She was, as far as anyone who studied her could tell, doing everything she was supposed to be doing. She was eating. She was growing. She had already survived one run-in with human machinery. She was inside a feeding ground that her species has used, as best we can reconstruct, for a very long time. And she was killed by a ship whose captain probably never even knew she was there.

---

There is a thing that happens with whale strandings in the internet era, which is that a dead whale on a public beach becomes, very quickly, a piece of content. People go. People take pictures. People post. I understand the impulse, because I had it, and I drove down the Park road to act on it. A whale is the largest animal any of us will ever see in person. Seeing one is, in some base and unexamined sense, *an event*, and events are things modern people document.

But a whale that has just died is not an event. She is a body. She is an animal who has a species and a sex and an age and a set of scars that tell you, if you know how to read them, what she has been through and what has been done to her. She deserves to be treated as what she was, which was a wild animal, killed accidentally, being handled by people whose job it is to handle her.

The impulse to get closer, to look, to take the picture, is not evil. But it is something we can notice ourselves having, and we can choose — I chose, on the morning of the second of April, without really deciding to — to do otherwise. To stop at the rope. To stand there. To leave.

There will be more of these. That is almost certain. The UME has not ended. The ships are not slowing down. The whales are increasingly in the same water, in the same months, at the same depths. Some future April morning there will be another young female on the beach at North Beach or Gunnison or Spermaceti Cove, and another team in Tyvek suits, and another rope, and another person from the Highlands standing at that rope with a camera in her hand.

If you are that person — if you are ever that person — I would ask you, as a neighbor who has been that person, to consider not taking the photograph.

Stand there.

Notice what has happened.

And then go home.
