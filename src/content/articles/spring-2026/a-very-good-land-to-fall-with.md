# A Very Good Land to Fall With

*In September 1609, an English mariner wrote the first known description of the Navesink Highlands. The land has been answering back ever since.*

The coastline had been low for days. The pine barrens of what would one day be called New Jersey slid past in a long, flat green line — unremarkable to a ship's company that had spent four months at sea and was starting, in the way that ship's companies do, to lose its patience with the continent itself. Then, early on the morning of the second of September, 1609, the lookout aboard a small Dutch vlieboot called the Halve Maen saw something rise up out of the haze.

Hills. Real ones. Two hundred and sixty-six feet at their highest point — modest by any measure that mattered to men raised in sight of the Pennines or the Harz, but to a crew coming off a transatlantic passage and searching for high ground by which to fix their position on the coast of a continent they did not yet have a name for, they were a mercy. A landmark. A thing you could set a course by.

The first mate, an Englishman named Robert Juet, kept the log. His entry for that day is terse, as ship's logs are, but something in it gives way at the end. "This," he wrote, "is a very good Land to fall with, and a pleasant Land to see."

Four hundred and seventeen years later, on clear days, you can still see what he saw.

---

The Halve Maen — the Half Moon, in English — was not supposed to be there. She was a three-masted flyboat of perhaps eighty-five tons, built for shallow coastal trade, and she had been contracted that spring by the Dutch East India Company to find a shortcut to Asia. Her captain was an Englishman in Dutch employ, Henry Hudson, forty-some years old, already twice unsuccessful at locating a Northeast Passage over the top of Russia. His orders on this third voyage had been specific: go north, go east, come home. Do not, under any circumstances, sail west.

Hudson sailed west.

He had his reasons — ice, mutiny, weather, ambition, it depends on which historian you ask — and by the end of July his ship was off the coast of what would later be called Maine, and by mid-August she was off the Chesapeake, and on the last day of that month she turned north again, coasting the low continent, looking for an opening. The opening, Hudson thought, might be a river wide enough and deep enough to slip through a whole country and come out the other side. He had been told about such a possibility by his friend Captain John Smith, who had sent him a letter and a rough chart.

What he was looking for, in other words, was a way through. What he found instead was the Navesink.

---

It helps to understand what "falling with" meant to a mariner in 1609.

To fall with a land was to come upon it at sea — to raise it above the horizon and identify it and know, with some measure of relief, where you were. The verb is archaic now. It survives in the related nautical phrase *landfall*, which still carries a faint residue of the original meaning: the moment a voyage stops being abstract. The coast becomes a specific coast. The continent becomes a place.

For a sailor approaching the mouth of what would later be called New York Harbor, the problem was this: for hundreds of miles in either direction, there was nothing tall. The Jersey shore is sand and low marsh. The Long Island shore is the same. A navigator working from dead reckoning alone could easily overshoot his entrance by fifty miles and never know it. He needed a mark. A piece of geography that behaved, from offshore, the way a cathedral spire behaves over the rooftops of a town — a thing that told you, even before you were close enough to resolve it, that you had arrived.

The Navesink Highlands are that mark.

Rising abruptly from the Atlantic coastal plain just south of where the Shrewsbury and the Navesink empty into Sandy Hook Bay, they are, by several measures, the highest natural point on the Atlantic seaboard south of Maine. Mount Mitchill, the highest of them, is two hundred and sixty-six feet above sea level. That sounds small. It is not. Stand on Mount Mitchill on a clear October afternoon and you can see forty miles in three directions. From the deck of a sixteenth-century ship, drawing perhaps twelve feet of water and moving six knots on a west wind, you could see the Highlands well before you could see the beach. They were the announcement that the harbor was there.

Juet, who was a difficult man — pessimistic, quarrelsome, eventually implicated in the mutiny that would kill Hudson on a later voyage — wrote about them with what reads, even across four centuries of distance, as genuine relief.

---

The Halve Maen anchored inside Sandy Hook on the third of September, in what would today be the waters just off Horseshoe Cove. For ten days she stayed in the lower harbor. Hudson's men took soundings. They fished. They went ashore. They encountered the people who had been living on that coast for at least ten thousand years — the Navesink, a band of the Lenape, whose name now attaches to the river and the highlands and a great many streets, schools, and yacht clubs in present-day Monmouth County, and whose descendants, in a quiet and complicated way, are still here.

The first meeting was cordial. Juet's journal records an exchange of gifts — tobacco, corn, dried currants, beads, a knife. The Lenape paddled out in dugout canoes and came aboard the ship and looked at it and went home. They returned. Hudson's men went ashore and looked at the land and came back. For several days, the two groups moved carefully around each other in the way that strangers will.

On the sixth of September, a small expedition from the Halve Maen, pulling an open boat upriver to scout, was attacked. It is not clear why. An arrow struck one of Hudson's English sailors, John Coleman, through the throat, and he died before they could return him to the ship. They buried him the next morning on what is believed to have been a point of sand somewhere near the present-day entrance to the harbor — possibly Sandy Hook itself, possibly Coney Island. No one knows. The burial is the first recorded death of a European on the soil of what would become New York, and it is also, almost certainly, the first recorded death of a European inside the boundary of the land we now call home.

The Halve Maen weighed anchor on the eleventh of September and sailed up the river that would carry Hudson's name.

---

Juet's phrase — *a very good Land to fall with* — had no afterlife in his lifetime. The journal was an internal document. It was used, eventually, by Dutch and English cartographers, and quoted here and there in early colonial accounts, but the Highlands acquired their formal name from other sources: from Lenape words, from Dutch surveyors, from the Hartshorne family's 1680 deed.

The phrase has become, in our century, a local secret. You will find it painted on the window of a restaurant in Atlantic Highlands. You will hear it in a lecture at the Twin Lights State Historic Site. You will see it on the letterhead of a preservation nonprofit. It has the quality, now, of an incantation — a way of insisting that what was seen by the first European eyes to see it remains, substantially, itself.

And in some ways it does. The Highlands are still here. They are publicly owned, most of them — Hartshorne Woods Park, Mount Mitchill Scenic Overlook, Gateway National Recreation Area at Sandy Hook. The forest that Juet saw was chestnut and oak; the forest you will see today is mostly oak and tulip and a great deal of understory that his crew would not have recognized. But the shape of the land has not changed. The way it rises out of the water has not changed. The view from the top, on a clear day, remains one of the most astonishing in the country, and it is, as far as we can tell, the same view.

Stand on Mount Mitchill on an afternoon in early May. Face east. The ocean will be the color of hammered tin. Sandy Hook will lie in a long pale curve to your left. The Atlantic Highlands lighthouse, the Twin Lights, will sit in their stone doubleness just south of you. Far out, in the shipping lanes, a container vessel will be coming in from Europe or leaving for Brazil. And if you turn your back on all of that and look at the land itself — the folded woods, the quiet neighborhoods, the steep streets falling away toward the river — you will be looking at something that, in its essential contours, Robert Juet would recognize.

He would recognize it, and he would write it down, and he would be right.

It is a very good land to fall with.

It is a pleasant land to see.
