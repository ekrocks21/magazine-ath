# Return

The first sign this year came in early March — a single osprey over the Shrewsbury, working low the way they do when they are hunting and not yet nesting. I watched it from the kitchen window, through the steam of the kettle, while the hill below the house held its winter silence. It circled once and kept going. Gone.

By the end of the month there were more. Two on the Navesink platform. One at the Highland Beach pole that has held a nest, off and on, for as long as I have been looking. The air had not changed much. But they had felt something I could not.

This is the rhythm of the coastal year, and it is the reason this magazine exists. Each season brings its own traffic to Sandy Hook — its own migrations, its own light, its own returning regulars. In spring it is the ospreys first, then the gannets still passing north in great diagonal lines offshore, then the striped bass pushing up the Shrewsbury behind the herring, then the horseshoe crabs coming in on the full moon tides to a beach they have been coming to for four hundred and forty-five million years. The humans arrive too, in their own order. First the charter captains, uncovering their boats behind Bahrs. Then the weekenders. Then, one morning in June, you realize the ferry is running again and the season is on.

There is a particular kind of listening that living here trains you to do. Not the dramatic listening of winter storms, when the glass rattles and the ocean audibly rearranges the beach. Something quieter. Knowing when the gannets have left. Hearing, in the pilings at Pews Creek, the shift from cormorant to egret. The first time the wind comes out of the south warm instead of cold. The first mosquito. The day the gulls stop standing on the road and start standing in the dune grass.

This issue is about that listening. The Spring 2026 Local Lookout carries a feature on Robert Juet, who was the first mate of the Halve Maen in 1609 and who wrote down the first known description of these Highlands in any European language. It carries a story about Richard Hartshorne, who bought Sandy Hook in 1680 for thirteen shillings, and an essay on Building 600, the house that has somehow survived every attempt to erase it. Jay Cosgrove talks about four generations at Bahrs. A humpback came ashore at Sandy Hook this winter, and we went and looked, and this issue tells you what we saw.

Above the Hook began, as most things do, with a small conviction — that this coastline had stories that were being forgotten, and that telling them was useful work. Four issues in, that conviction has only deepened. The ospreys have come back. So have we.

*— Eric, from the hill*
